Origin

Calisthenics ,which comes from the Ancient Greek Word kalós (καλός) and sthenos (σθένος) which means beautiful and strength, is a form of exercise that strives to achieve full body mastery through the use of bodyweight movements. It consists of various movements from different disicplines, although majority of advanced skills are taken from gymnastics which some would argue is technically counted as Calisthenics as well.

Back when it first originated, it was the go to way of exercising. However, when people invented weights and so on, weightlifting became the go to way of getting stronger. However, in the past few years, calisthenics has been slowly gaining traction again. Part of the reason was also because of Covid-19 which made going to the commerical gym not a viable option for many people.

Why Calisthenics?

Calisthenics is not only a good way to get fit, it also would teach you body control and awareness.
Not only that, Calisthenics can essentially be done anywhere since it requires little to no equipment at all compared to traditional weightlifting.
Not only is it convenient, it is also a safer choice considering the situation nowadays with Covid-19.

How to Start?

Build a Solid Foundation


Calisthenics is similar to building a house. To last long, a solid foundation is necessary. Not only would it make future progress easier when you have a solid foundation, it also greatly reduces the risks of injuries when you attempt harder skills. Thus, when you first start off, you should focus primarily on the basic. Only after mastering the basics like pushups, pullups and so on, can you start working on and focus on more advanced skills like the Planche, Front Lever, Handstand Pushup and many more.

Quality and Consistency


Quality and Consistency would take you very far. If you are super hardcore but only for a few months, that's worst than someone who trains less hard but more consistent. Consistency is what will give you life long results. You may have heard the saying "Practice makes perfect", however I argue that "Perfect practice makes perfect". If your practice is bad, you would be good at being bad. Thus, quality of each rep of your exercises matters more than the amount.
